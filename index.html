The Druggable Cleft:
Why a PET Tracer Sees Alzheimer's Tau but Not CTE — and How to Read the Difference

Interactive demo · Southworth Lab, UCSF · Data: PDB 8UQ7, 6NWP, 8FUG (cryo-EM) + live RCSB queries

A tau fibril is not one thing. The same protein folds into structurally distinct amyloid cores in different diseases — the paired-helical filament (PHF) of Alzheimer's, the Type-I fold of chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), the flat fold of PSP. Each is a different "strain," and each presents a different molecular surface. This is the central insight the Southworth lab has helped establish through cryo-EM: disease identity is written into fibril conformation.

It matters clinically because PET imaging tracers — the molecules that let us see tau in a living brain — bind these folds selectively. The lab's 2024 structure of the second-generation tracer MK-6240 bound to Alzheimer's PHF (PDB 8UQ7, 2.31 Å) revealed exactly where it docks: a narrow cleft on the fibril surface lined by Gln351, Lys353, and Ile360. But MK-6240 is an Alzheimer's tracer. It does not reliably light up CTE tau. Why not — when both folds are built from the very same residues?

The open question this demo targets. The lab can now generate defined AD and CTE folds in vitro (the 2025 mini-prion system) and solve tracer-bound structures. The missing piece is a way to read the binding cleft across folds — to predict, from structure alone, which folds a given tracer will engage. Below, I take the real coordinates and measure the cleft directly.

In the Alzheimer's fold, residues 351/353/360 wrap tightly around MK-6240. Switching to CTE keeps the same residues but swings them apart — the cleft opens and the ligand footprint no longer fits snugly.

Figure 2 — Measuring the Cleft: a Structural Fingerprint

The visual impression can be made quantitative. The MK-6240 pocket is walled by two strands; the distance between them sets whether a rigid tracer fits. I measured Cα–Cα distances for the pocket-defining residue pairs in both folds, directly from the deposited coordinates.

Pocket Cα–Cα distances · computed from PDB 8UQ7 (AD) and 6NWP (CTE)

The cleft-defining Gln351–Ile360 span opens from 18.1 Å in Alzheimer's to 21.4 Å in CTE — a 3.2 Å widening of the exact pocket MK-6240 occupies. A tracer optimized to bridge 18 Å loses its grip at 21 Å. This is a concrete, measurable structural basis for fold selectivity — and a design axis: a CTE-selective tracer would need a longer rigid spacer.

It is the same pipeline I would build to screen the growing library of strain and tracer-bound structures for their pocket geometry.

Put together, these three views trace a single idea: strain-specific folds create strain-specific clefts, the clefts are measurable, and the measurements can be automated across every structure the lab deposits. The natural next experiment — pairing the mini-prion system's defined AD and CTE fibrils with a panel of tracers and reading each pocket as it forms — is exactly the kind of structure-to-function analysis I would want to contribute to.
