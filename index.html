The Southworth lab has built one of the largest collections of tau fibril structures solved by cryo-EM. A single idea runs through much of that work: the same tau protein folds into different shapes in different diseases, and the shape is what decides how the fibril behaves. PET imaging tracers make this concrete. A tracer that lights up Alzheimer's tau in a brain scan can stay dark in another tauopathy, even though the protein sequence is identical.

I wanted to see how much of that difference can be read straight from the deposited coordinates, with no new experiment. Below are two short analyses I ran on the lab's published structures, followed by a small tool that repeats the method on any structure in the PDB.

The question these analyses target

The lab's 2024 structure of the tracer MK-6240 bound to Alzheimer's tau (PDB 8UQ7) shows exactly where the molecule sits: a shallow pocket formed by residues Gln351, Lys353, and Ile360. The obvious follow-up is whether that same pocket exists in other folds. If it collapses or opens too wide in the CTE fold, that would explain why an Alzheimer's tracer does not carry over. With the mini-prion system the lab published in 2025, defined AD and CTE fibrils can now be made on demand, so this comparison is no longer just a thought experiment. It is something the lab can test directly.

Analysis 1 — Does the MK-6240 pocket survive in the CTE fold?

Method
Projecting the ordered tau core from two deposited structures and overlaying the tracer

I took the Cα coordinates for the ordered core of tau (residues 305 to 379) from the Alzheimer's structure (8UQ7) and the CTE Type I structure (6NWP), projected each onto the plane facing down the fibril axis, and placed the MK-6240 ligand from the Alzheimer's model into the view. Analysis 2 — Putting a number on the pocket

Method
Cα–Cα distances across the pocket, measured from the deposited models

A picture is easy to over-read, so I measured it. The pocket is bounded by two strands, and the gap between them decides whether a rigid molecule can bridge it. I computed the same set of distances in both folds from the deposited coordinates.

Pocket Cα–Cα distances · 8UQ7 (AD) vs 6NWP (CTE)

The distance across the mouth of the pocket, from Gln351 to Ile360, is 18.1 Å in the Alzheimer's fold and 21.4 Å in CTE. That is a 3.2 Å difference at the exact site MK-6240 occupies. A tracer shaped to fit an 18 Å gap has little to hold onto at 21 Å. This gives a simple, measurable reason for the selectivity, and it also points to what a CTE-directed tracer would likely need: a longer rigid core to span the wider opening.

The same method on any structure

Tool
Live cross-section from any tau-filament PDB entry

The measurement above is not specific to two structures. Enter any PDB ID and this tool fetches the coordinates from the RCSB, pulls out the ordered core, computes the cross-section by principal-axis projection in the browser, and draws the fold. It is the same routine I would want to run across every structure the lab deposits, rather than comparing them one at a time by hand.

Try 8FUG (AD with tracer GTP-1), 6NWQ (CTE Type II), 8UQ7 (AD with MK-6240), 6NWP (CTE Type I).

Why this matters for the Southworth lab

The three views share one idea. Different folds produce different pockets, the pockets can be measured, and the measurement can be run across every structure the lab deposits without waiting for new data. Paired with the mini-prion fibrils, this becomes a way to compare candidate tracers against defined folds before committing microscope time, and to flag which folds a given tracer is unlikely to reach.

What I would bring

My background is in structural and computational analysis: parsing coordinate files, geometry and distance calculations, and building small tools that turn a repeated manual comparison into something reproducible. I would be glad to help build a pocket-comparison pipeline across the lab's tau structures, and to extend it to the tracer-bound and mini-prion datasets as they grow. The questions the lab works on, strain-specific folds and the molecules that read them, are exactly the kind of problem where careful analysis of the coordinates adds real value.

Methods note

Coordinates were downloaded from the RCSB PDB (8UQ7, 6NWP, 8FUG). Cross-sections were produced by principal-axis projection of the Cα atoms of one fibril layer. Distances are Cα to Cα, taken directly from the deposited models. Pocket residues are those with a Cα within 8 Å of the MK-6240 ligand in 8UQ7. The live tool fetches coordinates from the RCSB and runs the same projection in the browser. No new experimental data was generated.
